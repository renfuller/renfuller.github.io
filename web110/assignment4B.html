This week's assignment about Web Security was amusingly nostalgic. It reminded me of the internet safety lessons we used to have in the school library back in middle school. Even though I had heard some of this information before, it was nice getting a refresher because it's just as important today. For my two activities, I completed LearnFree's and TutorialPoint's Internet Safety courses. The first lessons focused on creating strong passwords. I learned that longer passwords with a mix of numbers, symbols, and uppercase letters are better than simple words or personal information. The courses also recommended using phrases or acronyms instead of birthdays or names. The next section covered web browsers' built-in security features, and this one especially hit home for me because I'm already pretty conscious about keeping my browser secure. I mainly use Opera GX with its built-in VPN, and I also have Malwarebytes installed on my PC. Between those two, I usually get warned before I visit a suspicious website or accidentally download something unsafe. Malwarebytes has been especially useful because it can scan my computer for malicious files that sometimes stick around even after you think you've deleted them. The last important reminder was about email scams and phishing. This is something I actually recognize from helping my grandfather sort through suspicious emails that were pretending to be from his health insurance provider. It reminded me how convincing these scams can look if you aren't paying close attention. It's important to never give away personal or sensitive information through email, and to avoid clicking links unless you're absolutely sure the sender is who they claim to be. These tips are pretty invaluable for anyone who uses the internet, but they especially hit home for me because I've downloaded a lot of mods over the years. Modding communities can be amazing, but they also tend to have shady download buttons, poorly archived websites, fake mirrors, and sketchy ads trying to trick you into downloading the wrong file. Experiences like that have taught me how important it is to keep good security software installed and always have backups of important files. Even if you think you're being careful, mistakes happen, so it's better to be prepared than to lose important data or end up with malware on your computer. Overall, this activity reminded me that good internet security isn't just about having antivirus software. It's also about building good habits, like using strong passwords, paying attention to browser security warnings, recognizing phishing attempts, and being cautious about what you click.
